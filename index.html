Classical Hollywood Era (1927-1969)

The characteristics of Classical Hollywood

A main struggle to solve a goal
A clear victory or defeat at the end of the movie
A conflict with other people or external circumstances

Hollywood's golden age came to an end in the 1960s when the actors from that era either retired or passed away. The rivalry from more popular TV and foreign films were other factors in the end of Hollywood's golden age.

The big 5 of Classical Hollywood

20th Century Fox
Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer
Paramount Pictures
RKO Pictures
Warner Bros.

The majority of production, distribution, and marketing companies were owned by these five studios. Each of them being vertical integrated.

The Supreme Court declared that Hollywood had turned into an oligarchy in 1948, ruling against the Big Five. These businesses dominated the film industry prior to this. Due to the big five's ownership of everything, smaller directors were unable to produce and distribute their films.

The small 3 of Classical Hollywood

columbia pictures
universal pictures
united artists

Despite not having the same theatre chains as the "Big Five," the "Little Three" studios played a significant role in Hollywood's Golden Age. These businesses weren't as large as the Big 5.

Alfred Hitchcock (1899-1980) was a British-American film director. known for his groundbreaking psychological thrillers and detailed storytelling, He started his career in the British film industry before going to Hollywood where he made movies such as Psycho, Vertigo, Rear Window and North by Northwest. His films frequently contain themes of false charges, complex characters, and twist endings, producing a distinct cinematic style that has influenced other filmmakers. Despite getting five Academy Award nominations for Best Director he never won, but he was honoured with the Irving G. Thalberg Memorial Award in 1968.
